1. Demografischer Wandel

    In den nächsten Jahren wird die Babyboomergeneration in Rente gehen und nach gemütlichen Altersruhesitzen im Grünen suchen. Der junge, überwiegend akademische Nachwuchs kann die freigewordenen Stellen in Engpassberufen des Handwerks und der Industrie nicht ausreichend ersetzen. Die Unternehmen reagieren mit attraktiven Arbeitsbedingungen; ein Pull-Faktor für die Region Kassel. Den (Klima)flüchtlingen wird die Ausübung von Engpassberufen als Bedingung für ihren Aufenthalt gestellt. Im ZRK bleibt die Einwohnerzahl bis 2050 wegen des Zuzugs konstant, der sich aber überwiegend auf die urbanen Quartiere konzentriert.

  2. 1870-1900

    Railway Expansion

    Between 1870 and 1900, railways add more than 150,000 miles of tracks within the United States, fueling industry, commerce, and travel. Coal-burning trains, along with the coal-burning factories they supplied, contribute to air pollution.

  3. 1883

    First Civil Rights Cases

    The U.S. Supreme Court hears the Civil Rights Cases of 1883, a group of similar cases that related to Black Americans being refused entry to privately owned, public areas such as hotels and trains. The Court rules that private business owners can choose to deny entry or service to Black patrons and that the 13th and 14th Amendments do not apply to privately owned businesses. This ruling reinforces Black Codes and opens the door to the Jim Crow laws that allow segregation of Black Americans.

    Learn more
  4. 1900-1920

    Immigration and Urban Growth

    The United States experiences rapid growth in early twentieth century. More than 15 million people immigrate to the U.S. between 1900 and 1915 alone, mostly from non-English speaking counties in Eastern and Southern Europe. Jobs in factories are plentiful and attract many immigrants seeking work. Many rural Americans also emigrate to cities seeking work in urban factories, many of which use coal to power manufacturing equipment.

  5. 1920-1929

    The Roaring 20s

    In the “Roaring Twenties,” consumer goods are plentiful and affordable, leading to mass spending. Relatively easy credit allows even ordinary people the chance to buy. The U.S. continues its pivot from having a majority rural population to an urban one. Much of the growth of cities is fueled by the Great Migration of thousands of Black Americans from the rural South to northern cities. Prosperity is the standard in the Roaring 20s until September 1929, when the stock market starts to falter. On October 29, Black Tuesday, the stock market crashes.

    Learn more
  6. 1929

    Great Depression Begins

    The Great Depression follows the stock market crash, and the Roaring 20s end with a whimper, as millions of Americans lose their livelihoods and their wealth. On March 4, 1933, Franklin Delano Roosevelt is sworn in as president. During his first hundred days in office he implements a series of economic stimulus programs called The New Deal helping to stabilize the economy, create jobs, and provide struggling Americans with economic relief. These programs are on the whole racially discriminatory, providing a far more robust safety net to White Americans than to Black Americans.

  7. 1933

    HOLC

    The Home Owners Loan Corporation (HOLC) program, part of The New Deal stimulus package, starts assisting low and moderate income homeowners already in foreclosure. The agency directly refinances homes, acquiring mortgages from struggling banks using government-guaranteed bonds. HOLC helps hundreds of thousands of homeowners by refinancing those mortgages. In contrast to previous types of mortgages from local lenders, such as ballooning or high-interest, short-term loans, HOLC issues loans that are typically 15-year, 5% interest amortizing loans.

    Learn more
  8. 1934-1938

    FHA

    The Federal Housing Administration (FHA), created as part of The National Housing Act of 1934, starts working with home lenders by providing mortgage insurance backed by the government on bank-issued mortgages. The FHA helps guard banks against losses if the borrower can't make payments. The FHA declines providing mortgage insurance in areas where racial and ethnic minorities reside. In turn, banks hesitate to issue loans that are not backed by the FHA. In 1937, the HOLC starts publishing maps using green, blue, yellow, and red colors to rate neighborhoods, an explicit example of the discriminatory and segregationist policies of federal housing policy.

    Learn more
  9. 1951

    HOLC Ceases Operation

    The HOLC ceases operations 15 years after issuing the last of over 1 million refinancing loans between 1933 and 1936. Between 1936 and 1951, the HOLC collected payments on mortgages and closes its doors leaving a small surplus to the U.S. Treasury.

    Learn more
  10. 1964-1968

    Civil Rights Movement

    After years of activism and work by Black Americans and some White allies in the Civil Rights Movement, in 1964 President Johnson signs into law the Civil Rights Act. The act prohibits segregation in public places that provide accommodations such as parks, hotels, and restaurants. In 1968, the Fair Housing Act is passed, prohibiting discrimination in the housing market based on their race, sex, skin color, nationality, or religion. In 1988, the Act is amended to include family status and disability. State and local laws may include further protections.

  11. 1977

    Community Reinvestment Act

    The Community Reinvestment Act, a federal law meant to encourage institutions to meet the financial and credit needs of the people in low and moderate income areas is enacted. The act is primarily intended to help mitigate some of the effects of discriminatory lending practices.

  12. 2021

    Today

    Over 75 years after the HOLC maps were created, neighborhoods that were undervalued and rated as red (grade D), the lowest rating for desirability when compared to other areas in these cities, continue to experience the legacy of redlining. That legacy includes lower household income, less intergenerational wealth, and higher rates of health issues such as asthma and pre-term birth. The ongoing disinvestment in residential real estate coupled with investment in industries and commerce within or near these neighborhoods have contributed to environmental vulnerabilities such as higher amounts of impervious surfaces, urban heat islands, and pollution.
